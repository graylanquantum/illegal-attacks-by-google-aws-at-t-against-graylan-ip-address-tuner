## III. AUTHORITIES FOR EMERGENCY ACTION

### A. Defense Production Act (DPA)

The DPA (50 U.S.C. ¬ß¬ß4501 et seq.) empowers the Executive to **prioritize and allocate** materials, services, and facilities essential to national defense and to mitigate supply chain or infrastructure disruptions. **50 U.S.C. ¬ß4511** authorizes the President to require priority performance of contracts or orders relating to **scarce, critical, and essential** materials and services; it also frames findings necessary for such priority orders. Critically, ¬ß4511 includes limiting language: priority authority may not be used unless the President finds the materials, services, or facilities are **‚Äúscarce, critical, and essential‚Äù** to maintain essential systems; further, Congress expects transparent findings and (when applicable) notifications for significant acquisitions. The NEA provides procedural scaffolding for declaring and managing national emergencies and for activating specific statutory powers, including those in the DPA. ### C. Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA)

The CFAA (18 U.S.C. ¬ß1030) criminalizes unauthorized access to protected computers, transmission of harmful code, and intentional damage. It provides for criminal penalties and civil remedies. Forensic preservation and investigation under this statute are warranted where there is credible evidence of injected payloads, unauthorized VM deployment, or targeted exploitation, especially if the affected system is connected to interstate or foreign commerce (nearly always true of internet-connected devices). ([Legal Information Institute][1])

## V. CONSTITUTIONAL ANALYSIS (FIRST AMENDMENT TRACK)

### A. The ‚ÄúModern Public Square‚Äù and Its Limits

In **Packingham v. North Carolina (2017)**, the Supreme Court recognized social media as ‚Äúone of the most important places‚Äù for speech, analogizing to a **modern public square**. The Court struck down a law broadly restricting access to social networks, emphasizing the critical role these platforms play in contemporary discourse. This case supplies persuasive language for framing social platforms as central to First Amendment interests‚Äîeven if the platforms are privately owned. ([Supreme Court][3], [Harvard Law Review][4])

However, **Manhattan Community Access Corp. v. Halleck (2019)** clarified that **private** entities operating forums for speech are **not generally state actors** subject to First Amendment constraints. Unless a platform is performing a **traditional and exclusive** public function or is coerced to act by the government, its editorial choices remain private conduct. This significantly cabins claims that platform moderation alone is a constitutional violation. ([Supreme Court][12], [Oyez][13])

### B. State Action Doctrine, ‚ÄúJawboning,‚Äù and Coercion

For private moderation to raise First Amendment issues, **state action** must be shown via coercion, significant encouragement, joint participation, or the performance of an exclusive public function. Several precedents outline these contours:

* **Bantam Books v. Sullivan (1963):** State ‚Äúadvisories‚Äù pressuring distributors to suppress books were unconstitutional; persuasive power plus implicit threats constituted de facto censorship. ([Library of Congress Tiles][8], [Justia Law][7])
* **Norwood v. Harrison (1973):** The state may not **induce, encourage, or promote** private persons to do what the state is constitutionally forbidden to do; state subsidies cannot facilitate unconstitutional discrimination. ([Justia Law][14], [The Free Speech Center][9])
* **O‚ÄôHandley v. Weber (9th Cir. 2023):** The court held that Twitter‚Äôs moderation after a state official flagged content did not transform Twitter into a state actor absent coercive control or ceding of authority. This reinforces that mere communication between government and platform is typically **insufficient** to establish state action. ([Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals CDN][5], [Electronic Frontier Foundation][6], [NetChoice][15], [Knight First Amendment Institute][16])

**Synthesis:** To claim a First Amendment violation based on de-platforming of a political figure, one would need evidence of **government coercion or significant encouragement** (the ‚Äújawboning‚Äù theory) or a showing that platforms performed a traditional public function on the state‚Äôs behalf. Otherwise, under **Halleck**, platforms retain private editorial autonomy. This does not mean the harms are trivial; it means the **constitutional hook** is contingent on **state involvement**.

### C. Reputational and Democratic Harms from De-platforming a Major Political Figure

Even where the formal First Amendment claim falters on state-action grounds, the **policy and democratic harms** are acute. The ban of a leading political figure can:

1. **Fracture the communicative link** between a candidate and millions of supporters, impairing the ability to **rapidly rebut allegations**, announce policy, and correct misinformation‚Äîclassic **reputational harm** in a compressed news cycle.
2. **Skew the marketplace of ideas** by removing one of the principal speakers from mainstream venues, raising public concerns about the **neutrality and legitimacy** of electoral discourse.
3. **Entrench gatekeeping power** in a handful of private actors who control quasi-essential conduits of public conversation‚Äîrisking **systemic bias** (perceived or real) that erodes public trust.

**Packingham**‚Äôs recognition of social media as a modern public square lends powerful **normative force** to the claim that **exclusion at scale** harms democratic deliberation‚Äîeven if, doctrinally, the platforms are private. The **dissonance** between the social reality of platforms as essential speech infrastructure and the legal reality that they‚Äôre private actors is a live policy problem, one that emergency management may address **temporarily** to ensure basic **access and stability** in periods of crisis, provided actions respect due process and are **narrowly tailored**. ([Supreme Court][3], [Harvard Law Review][4])
